Understanding the Retina

The retina captures light and sends visual signals to the brain. Diseases affecting it can cause blurred vision, distortion, or permanent vision loss — early intervention is essential to preserve sight.

What is the retina?

The light-sensitive layer behind your vision

The retina is a thin, light-sensitive layer of tissue located at the back of the eye. It captures light and converts it into electrical signals, which are transmitted to the brain through the optic nerve, allowing us to see.

Diseases affecting the retina can lead to blurred vision, distortion, or permanent vision loss if not diagnosed and treated promptly. Regular eye examinations and early intervention are essential for preserving retinal health.

Note: Sudden flashes, floaters, or a curtain-like shadow over your vision may indicate a retinal tear or detachment and require urgent evaluation.

What we treat

Common Retinal Conditions

Our retina specialists diagnose and treat a wide range of retinal disorders.

01

Diabetic Retinopathy

Diabetes-related damage to retinal blood vessels that may cause vision loss.

02

Age-Related Macular Degeneration

An age-related disease affecting the macula, leading to loss of central vision.

03

Retinal Detachment

The retina separates from the back of the eye, causing potential vision loss.

04

Retinal Tears & Holes

Breaks in the retina that may lead to detachment if untreated.

05

Macular Hole

An opening in the macula causing blurred and distorted central vision.

06

Epiretinal Membrane

Thin scar-like tissue on the retina that can cause visual distortion.

07

Retinal Vein Occlusion (RVO)

Blockage of retinal veins causing bleeding, swelling, and vision problems.

08

Central Serous Chorioretinopathy

Fluid accumulates under the retina, affecting central vision.

09

Vitreous Hemorrhage

Bleeding into the vitreous gel causing floaters and blurred vision.

10

Retinitis Pigmentosa

An inherited retinal disorder causing progressive loss of peripheral and night vision.

11

Retinal Artery Occlusion

A blockage in the retina's arterial blood supply, often causing sudden vision loss.

12

ROP & PVD

Retinopathy of Prematurity and Posterior Vitreous Detachment are also evaluated here.

Seek immediate attention if you notice

Symptoms of Retinal Disease

Common symptoms

  • Sudden or gradual loss of vision
  • Blurred or distorted vision
  • Straight lines appearing wavy
  • Poor night vision
  • Difficulty reading or recognizing faces

Urgent warning signs Seek care now

  • Floaters (spots, cobwebs, or black dots)
  • Flashes of light
  • A dark curtain or shadow over your vision
  • Loss of central or side vision
  • Sudden painless vision loss

Treatment for Retinal Conditions

Treatment depends on the specific retinal disease and its severity.

Comprehensive Retinal Evaluation

Dilated fundoscopy, OCT, Fundus Photography, and Fluorescein Angiography for accurate diagnosis.

Intravitreal Injections

Anti-VEGF and steroid injections treat DME, AMD, and retinal vein occlusions.

Retinal Laser Photocoagulation

Seals retinal tears and manages diabetic retinopathy and vascular disease.

Pneumatic Retinopexy

A minimally invasive procedure for selected cases of retinal detachment using a gas bubble.

Vitrectomy Surgery

An advanced microsurgical procedure used to treat retinal detachment, vitreous hemorrhage, macular hole, epiretinal membrane, severe diabetic eye disease, and retained intraocular foreign bodies.

What raises your risk

Causes & Risk Factors of Retinal Disorders

Diabetes

High blood sugar can damage retinal blood vessels and increase diabetic retinopathy risk.

Increasing age

Age-related changes raise the risk of macular degeneration and other retinal conditions.

High blood pressure

Can damage retinal blood vessels and affect retinal health.

Eye trauma

Injuries can cause retinal tears, detachment, or bleeding.

Family history

Genetic factors may increase the risk of certain retinal diseases.

High myopia

Severe nearsightedness can increase the risk of retinal tears and detachment.

Smoking

Increases the risk of age-related macular degeneration and retinal vascular problems.

Autoimmune conditions

Inflammatory or autoimmune conditions can affect the retina and surrounding structures.
