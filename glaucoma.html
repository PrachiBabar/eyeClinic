Understanding Glaucoma

Glaucoma is known as the "Silent Thief of Sight" — it damages the optic nerve gradually, often with no early symptoms. Regular screening is the only way to catch it before vision is lost.

What is glaucoma?

A quiet build-up of pressure that damages the optic nerve

Glaucoma is a group of eye diseases that damage the optic nerve, which carries visual information from the eye to the brain. This damage is often caused by increased pressure inside the eye, though glaucoma can also occur with normal eye pressure.

Left untreated, it can lead to permanent vision loss and blindness. Because it often develops without noticeable symptoms in its early stages, regular eye examinations are essential for early detection.

Note: Early-stage glaucoma usually has no symptoms — which is exactly why it's called the "Silent Thief of Sight."

Know the warning signs

Symptoms of Glaucoma

Symptoms vary depending on the type of glaucoma — some develop slowly, others strike suddenly and require emergency care.

Common symptoms

  • Gradual loss of peripheral (side) vision
  • Blurred vision
  • Difficulty seeing in low light
  • Frequent changes in vision

Acute glaucoma Medical emergency

  • Sudden, severe eye pain
  • Severe headache
  • Blurred vision
  • Halos around lights
  • Redness of the eye
  • Nausea and vomiting
  • Sudden loss of vision

If left untreated

Complications of Ignoring Glaucoma

Permanent vision loss

Optic nerve damage cannot be reversed, and lost vision cannot be restored.

Loss of peripheral vision

One of the earliest signs, making driving and walking difficult.

Tunnel vision

As the disease progresses, only central vision may remain.

Complete blindness

Advanced, untreated glaucoma can result in total, irreversible blindness.

Reduced quality of life

Vision loss affects reading, driving, work, and everyday independence.

Falls & injuries

Reduced peripheral vision makes navigation harder and raises accident risk.

Not all glaucoma is the same

Types of Glaucoma

01

Primary Open-Angle Glaucoma

The most common type. Develops slowly as the eye's drainage system becomes less efficient, raising eye pressure and gradually damaging the optic nerve.

02

Angle-Closure Glaucoma

Occurs when the drainage angle becomes blocked, causing a rapid rise in eye pressure. A medical emergency requiring immediate treatment.

03

Normal-Tension Glaucoma

Optic nerve damage occurs even though eye pressure stays within the normal range.

04

Congenital Glaucoma

A rare condition present at birth, caused by abnormal development of the eye's drainage system.

05

Secondary Glaucoma

Develops as a result of another eye condition, injury, inflammation, diabetes, or long-term steroid use.

Managing the condition

Treatment for Glaucoma

Vision already lost to glaucoma can't be restored, but early treatment can slow or prevent further damage.

Eye drops

Prescription drops that lower eye pressure by reducing fluid production or improving drainage.

Oral medications

In some cases, tablets are prescribed to further reduce eye pressure alongside drops.

Laser treatment

Procedures that improve fluid drainage or reduce fluid production, chosen based on your type and stage.

Selective Laser Trabeculoplasty (SLT)

A minimally invasive laser treatment for open-angle glaucoma that improves the eye's natural drainage and helps lower intraocular pressure.

YAG Peripheral Iridotomy (YAG PI)

Used primarily for angle-closure glaucoma. A tiny opening is created in the iris to improve fluid flow and relieve pressure inside the eye.

Optical Coherence Tomography (OCT)

High-resolution cross-sectional imaging of the retina and optic nerve.

  • Detects early retinal and optic nerve damage
  • Tracks disease progression
  • Guides diagnosis and treatment decisions

Visual Field Testing (Perimetry)

Evaluates central and peripheral (side) vision loss.

  • Detects functional vision changes
  • Monitors glaucoma progression and treatment effectiveness
  • Essential for long-term follow-up

When medication isn't enough

Glaucoma Surgeries

When medications and laser treatments can't sufficiently control glaucoma, surgery lowers eye pressure and protects the optic nerve.

Drainage

Trabeculectomy

Creates a new drainage pathway to allow excess fluid to leave the eye, reducing intraocular pressure.

Minimally invasive

MIGS

A group of advanced procedures that lower eye pressure through tiny incisions — faster recovery, favorable safety profile.

Implant

Ahmed Glaucoma Valve (AGV)

A drainage implant used in complex or advanced glaucoma cases to help control eye pressure.

Minimally invasive

GATT

Opens the trabecular meshwork from within to improve the eye's natural drainage.

Implant

iStent

A tiny implant that improves fluid outflow, often performed alongside cataract surgery.

Minimally invasive

BANG

Enhances aqueous outflow by creating an opening in the trabecular meshwork with a specially bent needle.

Around your surgery

Pre-Op, Post-Op & Recovery Care

  • 01Undergo a comprehensive glaucoma evaluation — eye pressure, OCT, visual field testing, and optic nerve assessment.
  • 02Inform your ophthalmologist about all medications, allergies, and existing medical conditions.
  • 03Follow instructions on blood thinners, diabetes medication, or other prescribed medicines.
  • 04Use all prescribed glaucoma eye drops and pre-operative medications as directed.
  • 05Avoid eating or drinking for the recommended period before surgery, if advised.
  • 06Arrange for someone to accompany you — you should not drive after the procedure.
  • 07Avoid eye makeup, creams, lotions, and perfumes on the day of surgery.
  • 01Use all prescribed eye drops and medications exactly as directed.
  • 02Wear the protective eye shield, especially while sleeping, for the recommended duration.
  • 03Do not rub, press, or touch the operated eye.
  • 04Avoid heavy lifting, strenuous exercise, and bending forward until advised by your doctor.
  • 05Keep water, soap, dust, and shampoo away from the operated eye during initial healing.
  • 06Wear sunglasses outdoors to protect your eyes from sunlight, dust, and wind.
  • 07Attend all scheduled follow-ups to monitor eye pressure and healing.
  • 08Contact your ophthalmologist immediately for severe pain, sudden vision loss, or unusual discharge.
  • 01Use medications exactly as prescribed — never skip a dose.
  • 02Wash your hands before applying eye drops.
  • 03Avoid smoking and dusty environments during recovery.
  • 04Resume normal activities only after your ophthalmologist's approval.
  • 05Never stop glaucoma medications unless specifically advised by your doctor.
  • 06Keep all follow-up appointments — long-term monitoring is essential for success.

Recovery varies by procedure. Mild redness, irritation, blurred vision, or a foreign-body sensation is common in the first few days and usually improves gradually.
