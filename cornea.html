What is the cornea?

The eye's clear, protective front window

The cornea is the clear, dome-shaped front surface of the eye that covers the iris and pupil. It plays a vital role in focusing light onto the retina, allowing us to see clearly.

Because the cornea is transparent and exposed to the environment, it can be affected by infections, injuries, degenerative diseases, and inherited conditions that may lead to blurred vision, discomfort, or vision loss if left untreated.

Good to know: Early diagnosis and appropriate treatment are essential to maintain healthy vision and prevent complications.

Common Corneal Conditions

Our specialists diagnose and treat a wide range of corneal disorders.

◎

Corneal Infections (Keratitis)

Bacterial, viral, or fungal infections affecting the cornea.

◎

Corneal Ulcers

Open sores on the cornea, often caused by infection or injury.

◎

Keratoconus

A thin, cone-shaped cornea causing progressively distorted vision.

◎

Corneal Scars & Dystrophies

Inherited or acquired changes that affect corneal clarity.

◎

Pterygium

A growth of tissue onto the cornea, sometimes called "surfer's eye."

◎

Recurrent Corneal Erosion

Repeated breakdown of the cornea's outer surface layer.

◎

Corneal Injuries & Foreign Bodies

Trauma or embedded particles affecting the corneal surface.

◎

Dry Eye Disease

Poor tear quality can damage the corneal surface over time.

When to see a specialist

Symptoms of Corneal Disease

Common symptoms

  • Blurred or decreased vision
  • Eye pain or discomfort
  • Redness of the eye
  • Excessive tearing
  • Sensitivity to light (Photophobia)

Also watch for

  • Foreign body sensation
  • Burning or irritation
  • White or cloudy spot on the cornea
  • Difficulty wearing contact lenses
  • Sudden vision changes

What raises your risk

Causes of Corneal Disorders

Eye infections

Bacterial, viral, fungal, or parasitic infections can damage the cornea.

Eye injuries

Trauma, scratches, burns, or foreign bodies can lead to damage and scarring.

Dry eye disease

Inadequate or poor-quality tears can damage the corneal surface.

Keratoconus

The cornea becomes thin and cone-shaped, causing distorted vision.

Corneal dystrophies

Inherited disorders that affect the clarity and function of the cornea.

Improper contact lens use

Poor lens hygiene or prolonged wear increases infection and ulcer risk.

Autoimmune diseases

Certain autoimmune conditions can cause inflammation and corneal damage.

Vitamin A deficiency

Can weaken the cornea and lead to vision-threatening complications.

Precision diagnostics

Advanced Technologies

Corneal Topography

Maps the shape, curvature, and thickness of the cornea to diagnose and monitor corneal disorders.

  • Detects and monitors Keratoconus and irregularities
  • Helps in planning LASIK and corneal procedures
  • Provides detailed assessment of structure and progression

OCT (Optical Coherence Tomography)

High-resolution cross-sectional images of the cornea for detailed layer-by-layer evaluation.

  • Evaluates corneal thickness and abnormalities
  • Assists in diagnosis and monitoring of corneal diseases
  • Helps in surgical planning and treatment follow-up

Matched to your condition

Treatment for Corneal Conditions

Treatment depends on the underlying cause and severity of the condition.

Comprehensive Corneal Evaluation

A detailed examination using advanced diagnostic equipment to assess corneal health and structure.

Medications

Antibiotic, antiviral, antifungal, anti-inflammatory, or lubricating drops for infection or dry eye.

Collagen Cross-Linking (C3R)

A minimally invasive procedure that strengthens the cornea and slows or stops keratoconus progression.

Specialty Contact Lenses

RGP, scleral, or custom lenses for irregular corneas, especially in keratoconus.

Pterygium Surgery

Surgical removal of abnormal tissue, often combined with a conjunctival autograft to reduce recurrence.

Corneal Transplantation

When the cornea is severely damaged, keratoplasty may be recommended.

Full-thickness

Penetrating Keratoplasty (PKP)

A complete, full-thickness corneal transplant.

Partial-thickness

DALK

Deep Anterior Lamellar Keratoplasty for diseases affecting the front corneal layers.

Selective

Endothelial Keratoplasty (DSEK/DMEK)

Selective replacement of the damaged inner corneal layer.
