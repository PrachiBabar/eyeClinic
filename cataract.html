Understanding Cataract

A cataract clouds the eye's natural lens, gradually blurring vision. It's one of the leading causes of vision impairment worldwide — and one of the most successfully treatable, with modern cataract surgery.

What is a cataract?

A cloudy lens that blurs the view

A cataract is a condition in which the natural lens of the eye becomes cloudy, leading to blurred or hazy vision. It usually develops gradually with age, but can also occur due to injury, certain medical conditions, medications, or congenital causes.

Cataracts are one of the leading causes of vision impairment — but they can be treated successfully with modern cataract surgery.

Good to know: Cataract surgery is one of the safest and most commonly performed surgeries in ophthalmology, with excellent visual outcomes.

Know the warning signs

Symptoms of Cataract

What you may notice

  • Blurred or cloudy vision
  • Difficulty seeing at night
  • Increased sensitivity to bright lights and glare
  • Seeing halos around lights
  • Frequent changes in eyeglass or contact lens prescription

As it progresses

  • Colors appearing faded or yellowish
  • Double vision in one eye
  • Difficulty reading or performing daily activities
  • Poor vision in bright sunlight

Not all cataracts form the same way

Types of Cataract

01

Age-Related Cataract

The most common type, developing naturally as part of the aging process.

02

Congenital Cataract

Present at birth or develops during childhood due to genetic or developmental factors.

03

Traumatic Cataract

Occurs after an eye injury and may develop immediately or years later.

04

Secondary Cataract

Develops due to diabetes, long-term steroid use, or following certain eye surgeries.

05

Nuclear Cataract

Forms in the center of the lens and commonly affects distance vision.

06

Cortical Cataract

Begins at the outer edge of the lens, often causing glare and difficulty with night vision.

Choosing your new lens

Intraocular Lens (IOL) Options

During surgery, the cloudy natural lens is replaced with an artificial lens — your specialist will help you choose the best fit for your lifestyle.

Monofocal Lens

Clear vision at one distance, usually for distance; reading glasses may still be needed.

EDOF Lens

Extended Depth of Focus — a continuous range of clear vision from distance to intermediate.

Multifocal Lens

Clear vision at both near and distance, reducing the need for glasses.

Trifocal Lens

Clear vision at near, intermediate, and distance for maximum spectacle independence.

Toric Lens

Corrects astigmatism while providing sharp, clear vision after surgery.

Not sure which lens fits you?

Your surgeon will recommend the best option based on your eyes and lifestyle.

Surgical techniques

Treatment Options for Cataract Surgery

Phacoemulsification (Phaco Surgery)

A modern cataract removal technique where the cloudy lens is broken into microscopic fragments using ultrasound energy and replaced with a clear artificial IOL. Small 2–3 mm incision, usually no stitches, quick and safe with faster visual recovery. The most widely performed cataract surgery technique worldwide.

MICS (Micro Incision Cataract Surgery)

An advanced technique performed through an ultra-small 1.8–2.2 mm incision, sutureless in most cases, with less surgical trauma, faster healing, and reduced surgically induced astigmatism.

Around your surgery

Pre-Op, Post-Op & Recovery Care

  • 01Undergo a comprehensive eye examination and biometric measurements for accurate IOL selection.
  • 02Inform your ophthalmologist about existing medical conditions, allergies, or medications.
  • 03Follow instructions on blood thinners and diabetes medications.
  • 04Use prescribed eye drops as directed before surgery.
  • 05Avoid eating or drinking for the recommended period before the procedure, if advised.
  • 06Arrange for someone to accompany you — you should not drive after surgery.
  • 07Avoid makeup, perfumes, lotions, or eye cosmetics on the day of surgery.
  • 01Use all prescribed eye drops exactly as directed.
  • 02Wear the protective eye shield, especially while sleeping, for the recommended duration.
  • 03Avoid rubbing or pressing on the operated eye.
  • 04Keep water, soap, shampoo, and dust away from the eye for the first few days.
  • 05Avoid strenuous exercise, heavy lifting, and bending forward for one to two weeks or as advised.
  • 06Wear sunglasses outdoors to protect your eyes from sunlight and dust.
  • 07Avoid swimming and hot tubs until your ophthalmologist confirms it's safe.
  • 08Contact your doctor immediately for severe pain, sudden vision loss, or increasing redness.
  • 01Take all medications exactly as prescribed.
  • 02Maintain good hand hygiene before using eye drops.
  • 03Avoid dusty environments during the early recovery period.
  • 04Resume routine activities only after your ophthalmologist's approval.
  • 05Follow all post-operative instructions carefully for the best visual outcome.

Most patients notice improved vision within 24–48 hours, though complete healing may take 4–6 weeks. Mild irritation, watering, or blurred vision is normal at first and improves as the eye heals.
