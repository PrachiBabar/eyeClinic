Understanding Diabetic Retinopathy

High blood sugar can damage the tiny blood vessels of the retina, and diabetic retinopathy often progresses silently. Regular diabetic eye screenings are the most reliable way to catch it early.

What is diabetic retinopathy?

A diabetes complication that targets the retina

Diabetic Retinopathy is a diabetes-related eye disease that affects the retina, the light-sensitive tissue at the back of the eye. High blood sugar levels can damage the tiny blood vessels in the retina, causing them to leak, swell, or become blocked.

If left untreated, it can lead to severe vision loss or blindness. Regular eye examinations are essential for early detection, especially for people with diabetes.

Note: Early diabetic retinopathy often has no symptoms, making regular diabetic eye screenings extremely important.

What you may notice

Symptoms of Diabetic Retinopathy

As the condition progresses

  • Blurred or fluctuating vision
  • Floaters (spots or dark strings in your vision)
  • Dark or empty areas in your field of vision
  • Difficulty reading or focusing
  • Poor night vision
  • Faded or distorted colors
  • Sudden vision loss in severe cases

How it progresses

Types of Diabetic Retinopathy

01

Mild NPDR

The earliest stage — tiny microaneurysms form in retinal blood vessels and may leak small amounts of fluid.

02

Moderate NPDR

Some retinal blood vessels become blocked, reducing blood supply to the retina.

03

Severe NPDR

Many blood vessels become blocked, reducing oxygen supply and signalling new vessel growth.

04

Proliferative DR (PDR)

The most advanced stage — fragile new vessels can bleed, cause retinal detachment, and permanent vision loss if untreated.

05

Diabetic Macular Edema (DME)

Fluid accumulates in the macula, the retina's central part responsible for sharp vision, causing blurred central vision.

If left untreated

Complications of Diabetic Retinopathy

Vitreous hemorrhage

Fragile abnormal vessels bleed into the vitreous, causing sudden floaters or severe vision loss.

Retinal detachment

Scar tissue pulls the retina away, causing flashes, floaters, and a curtain-like shadow.

Diabetic macular edema

Fluid in the macula causes blurred or distorted central vision.

Neovascular glaucoma

Abnormal vessels block drainage, causing painful eye pressure that damages the optic nerve.

Permanent vision loss or blindness

Without timely treatment, irreversible retinal damage can result in complete blindness.

Managing the condition

Treatment for Diabetic Retinopathy

Treatment depends on the stage and severity of the disease.

Blood Sugar & BP Control

Maintaining healthy blood sugar, blood pressure, and cholesterol is the first step in slowing progression.

Intravitreal Injections

Anti-VEGF or steroid injections reduce swelling and prevent abnormal blood vessel growth.

Laser Treatment

Seals leaking blood vessels or shrinks abnormal new vessels to reduce the risk of vision loss.

Vitrectomy Surgery

For advanced cases with significant bleeding or retinal detachment, to restore or preserve vision.

Regular Eye Examinations

Comprehensive dilated eye exams at least once a year, or more frequently if advised.

Optical Coherence Tomography (OCT)

Detailed cross-sectional imaging of the retina to detect and monitor diabetic retinal changes.

  • Detects Diabetic Macular Edema and retinal swelling
  • Evaluates retinal thickness and fluid accumulation
  • Monitors treatment response and disease progression

Fundus Photography

High-resolution images of the retina to identify and document diabetic eye changes.

  • Detects retinal bleeding, microaneurysms, and vessel changes
  • Helps in early diagnosis and regular monitoring
  • Documents treatment planning and follow-up care

  • 04Follow instructions regarding diabetes medications and other prescribed medicines.
  • 05Use prescribed eye drops before the procedure as advised.
  • 06Keep the eye area clean and avoid eye makeup on the day of treatment.
  • 07Arrange for someone to accompany you, especially if vision may be temporarily affected.
  • 01Use all prescribed eye drops and medications exactly as directed.
  • 02Avoid rubbing or pressing on the treated eye.
  • 03Maintain good hand hygiene before applying eye drops.
  • 04Avoid heavy lifting, strenuous activities, and bending forward until advised.
  • 05Protect your eyes from dust, smoke, and accidental injury.
  • 06Wear sunglasses outdoors for comfort and protection.
  • 07Monitor your blood sugar levels regularly to prevent disease progression.
  • 08Report immediately if you notice sudden vision loss, severe pain, or a sudden increase in floaters.
